1. Background:

Canada needs an energy agenda for 2026–2030 that supports four goals at the same time: (1) affordable energy for households and firms, (2) reliable energy supply, (3) lower greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, and (4) strong investment and jobs. Two SDG-aligned policy directions can move these goals: expanding renewable energy share (SDG 7.2.1) and reducing energy intensity (SDG 7.3.1).

SDG 7.2.1 measures the share of total final energy consumption that comes from renewable sources. SDG 7.3.1 tracks “energy intensity,” defined as energy supplied to the economy per unit of economic output (GDP). A lower energy intensity value generally means the economy uses less energy to produce one unit of value, although it is an imperfect proxy for pure efficiency (it can also reflect economic structure, climate, and the mix of activities).

Recent Canadian data shows why both levers matter. Statistics Canada reported that in 2023 overall energy use decreased by 0.5% while real GDP increased by 2.0%, and GHG emissions decreased by 0.9%. This pattern is consistent with improving energy intensity and/or a cleaner energy system. On the supply side, Canada’s electricity system already has a large renewable base. The Canada Energy Regulator reports renewable generation capacity increased from 63% of total capacity in 2010 to 69% in 2023, and its near-term outlook shows renewable capacity could reach about 73% by 2030.

#加拿大需要一项面向 2026–2030 年的能源议程，同时支持四个目标：（1）为家庭和企业提供可负担的能源，（2）可靠的能源供应，（3）降低温室气体（GHG）排放，以及（4）强劲的投资和就业。两项与可持续发展目标一致的政策方向可以推动这些目标：扩大可再生能源占比（SDG 7.2.1）和降低能源强度（SDG 7.3.1）。

SDG 7.2.1 衡量来自可再生来源的最终能源消费在总最终能源消费中的占比。SDG 7.3.1 跟踪“能源强度”，定义为供应给经济的能源与经济产出（GDP）单位的比值。较低的能源强度值通常意味着经济在生产单位价值时使用更少的能源，尽管它是对纯效率的一个不完全代理（它也可能反映经济结构、气候和活动构成）。

最近的加拿大数据说明了为何两个杠杆都很重要。加拿大统计局报告称，2023 年总体能源使用减少了 0.5%，实际国内生产总值增长了 2.0%，而温室气体排放减少了 0.9%。这种模式与能源强度的改善和/或更清洁的能源系统一致。在供应方面，加拿大的电力系统已经有较大的可再生能源基础。加拿大能源监管机构报告称，可再生发电装机容量从 2010 年占总装机容量的 63%增加到 2023 年的 69%，其短期展望显示可再生装机容量到 2030 年可能达到约 73%。

2. Key stakeholders
- Federal government (Natural Resources Canada and related departments): sets national priorities, funding, and standards.
- Provincial and territorial governments: plan power systems and run many efficiency programs.
- Electric utilities and grid operators: build and operate generation, transmission, and distribution.
- Oil and gas producers and energy-intensive industry: affected by policy direction, costs, and demand changes.
- Renewable energy developers and equipment suppliers: depend on project pipelines, permits, and grid access.
- Households and small businesses: experience energy bills, retrofit costs, and service reliability.
- Indigenous governments and communities: rights, benefits, and impacts tied to project siting and land use.
- Municipalities and regulators: local permitting, zoning, and community acceptance.
